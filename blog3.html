Why I Stopped Buying Kiln-Dried Lumber

By Elena Marsh Materials 5 min read

Air-dried wood moves differently, smells different, and cuts different. It's slower to source, harder to find in consistent sizes, and impossible to rush — which, it turns out, was the whole point I'd been missing.

Kiln drying exists to solve a supply-chain problem: get wood to a stable moisture content fast enough to sell it. It does that job well. What it doesn't do is preserve much of the character — the color, the smell, the way the fibers respond to a hand plane — that comes from a slower conversion from living tree to usable board.

The practical difference at the bench

The first time I planed a board of air-dried walnut, the shavings came off in long, waxy curls instead of the drier, flakier ribbons I was used to. The wood held an edge tool's cut cleanly instead of fuzzing at the surface. None of that shows up in a lumber yard's spec sheet.

Kiln-dried wood is reliable. Air-dried wood is alive in a way you can still feel with your hands.

What it costs you

  • Time: a rule of thumb is one year of air drying per inch of thickness.
  • Storage: you need a dry, ventilated space to sticker and stack boards properly.
  • Predictability: moisture content varies more, so you test each board before committing to a project.

None of that is convenient. All of it, for the kind of furniture I want to make, is worth it.
