The Case for Sharpening Your Own Chisels

By Elena Marsh Tools 6 min read

A dull chisel lies to you about the wood. It tells you the grain is fighting back, that the timber is harder than it looks, that you need more force. Almost none of that is true. What's actually happening is that a rounded, ragged edge is tearing fibers instead of severing them — and no amount of muscle fixes a geometry problem.

I used to think sharpening was a chore to get through before the "real" work started. It took me years of frustrating, splintery paring cuts to realize sharpening is the real work. Everything else is just moving a sharp edge through wood in the right direction.

What a sharp edge actually looks like

Under a loupe, a freshly honed edge is a clean meeting of two flat planes — no wire edge, no micro-serrations, no shine where there should be a line. You don't need a loupe to test it, though. A sharp chisel will shave the hair off your arm with almost no pressure, or slice a strip of paper cleanly without folding it.

If your chisel can't do either of those things, it isn't ready for the bench, no matter how new it looks.

The five-minute routine

  1. Flatten the back on your coarsest stone until it's mirror-flat near the edge — this only needs doing once, thoroughly, per chisel.
  2. Set your bevel angle and work through progressively finer grits, feeling for the wire edge to form and then refine.
  3. Strop on leather or a fine ceramic stone to remove the wire edge and polish the bevel.
  4. Test on end grain pine. If it shears cleanly with light pressure, you're done.
The stone doesn't care how expensive your chisel was. It only cares whether you show up to it regularly.

Buy one good chisel before you buy six mediocre ones, and buy a strop before you buy a second stone. Most of what separates a frustrating afternoon from a satisfying one isn't the tool — it's whether that tool was sharp when you picked it up.
