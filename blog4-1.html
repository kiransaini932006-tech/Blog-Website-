A Small Joinery Vocabulary Worth Learning

By Elena Marsh Technique 7 min read

You don't need forty joints to build well. You need four, learned properly: the dovetail, the mortise and tenon, the dado, and the rabbet. Everything else is a variation on one of these, dressed up for a specific situation.

The dovetail

Mechanically interlocking and resistant to being pulled apart, which is exactly what a drawer front needs when someone yanks it open too hard. It's also the joint most associated with "fine" woodworking, which has made it more intimidating than it needs to be. Start wide and forgiving before chasing the fine, delicate dovetails you see in photographs.

The mortise and tenon

The workhorse of frame construction — table legs to aprons, door rails to stiles. A snug mortise and tenon resists racking in every direction at once, which is why it's survived essentially unchanged for thousands of years of furniture making.

The dado

A channel cut across the grain to seat a shelf or divider. Unglamorous, extremely strong, and the reason a bookshelf holds its shelves level instead of relying on nails alone.

The rabbet

An L-shaped notch along an edge, most often seen holding cabinet backs or picture frames square. Simple to cut, easy to overlook, and quietly present in more finished pieces than beginners realize.

Learn these four on scrap wood until they're boring. Boring is what confidence feels like from the inside.

How to actually practice them

  1. Cut each joint on cheap pine before ever attempting it in your project wood.
  2. Dry-fit before glue, every time, no exceptions.
  3. When a joint fails, diagnose why before cutting the next one — most failures repeat until you name the cause.

Furniture doesn't need forty joints. It needs four you trust completely.
