Building a Workbench With Only Hand Tools

By Elena Marsh Projects 8 min read

No table saw, no router, no plunge of any kind — just a handsaw, a jack plane, and three weekends. I built my workbench this way on purpose, not out of romanticism but out of a practical problem: I didn't have a bench yet, and every power tool I owned needed one to be used safely.

So I started at the bottom of the tool chain instead of the top, and it turned out to be the better lesson anyway.

Why the bench comes first

A workbench isn't a surface — it's a system of holding. Every other skill in the shop assumes the wood is held still, square, and at a useful height. Skip that step and you spend your whole woodworking life fighting your own project just to keep it from sliding around.

I built mine from construction-grade fir, laminated in sections, with a simple leg vise on the front and a wagon vise at the tail. Nothing exotic. The species matters far less than the mass and the flatness.

What three weekends actually looked like

  • Weekend one: Rip and joint the top boards by hand, glue up in two batches to keep clamps manageable.
  • Weekend two: Flatten the glued-up top with a jack and then a jointer plane, cut the leg joinery.
  • Weekend three: Assemble the base, fit the vises, drill dog holes, apply finish.
A bench built slowly teaches you the shop's whole vocabulary before you've cut a single dovetail for a "real" project.

It isn't the fastest way to get a bench. It is, I'd argue, the fastest way to become the kind of woodworker who deserves one.
